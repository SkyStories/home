Overview/Synopsis

“There is what we know as real love.

And then, there’s what we know as a mere illusion.

And, still then, there’s me.”

        The story of the False Romeo—the failed lover who tries to impress and ends up with nothing—begins with our “hero,” Yuuto Ishikawa. A mediocre student and rather terrible in the relationship department, the 15-year-old Yuuto is forced into an unfamiliar high school after moving for the first time in his life to a city that he’s barely visited before.

        Never having been a “people person,” as he calls it, Yuuto finds himself in a bind at the school. Almost nobody talks to him (though that doesn’t bother him too much), few people pay him any attention and nobody expects anything of him.

        However, a week into the school year, a new transfer student joins his class, and introduces herself to him—as his fiancée, that is. Hanae Tsukuda, a girl Yuuto is completely unfamiliar with…or perhaps not? But the story wouldn’t be interesting if it ended there! Yuuto seems to also catch the eye of another girl in his class: Sayaka Hamasaki, the class representative for Yuuto’s class 1-C.

        Determined to make a change, Yuuto makes himself a promise and strives to force himself out of his comfort zone and become more socially accepted. That is where our story begins! Enter our False Romeo, side by side with us, trapped in this play, and a dance of eternal solitude…or so he thinks. Heaven knows, maybe there’s someone else waiting for him out there!

Format

This story is written in chapters, separated by the chapter number (in roman numerals, ‘cause why not?).

Yuuto quotes literature once in a while. These quotes are fully written in italics, but are also between quotation marks (“texttexttext texttexttext texttexttext”). You can probably guess what he’s quoting though.

Texts (on the phone) are done with single quotation marks (‘texttexttext texttexttext texttexttext’). They are also much less formal than normal conversation, as they are directly quoting the text. Regular conversation is done…well…regularly (“texttexttext texttexttext texttexttext”). Thoughts are done without quotation marks in italics (texttexttext texttexttext texttexttext). Quotations within text, such as saying something while using air quotes in real life, are done with single marks within quotation marks (“texttexttext ‘texttexttext’ texttexttext”)
